Parts of Speech Overview

Nouns

are people, places, things, and ideas. In Latin nouns have grammatical gender, number, and case.

Gender

can be Masculine (M), Feminine (F), or Neuter (N). Nouns have a gender that does not change. Often the gender will make sense. For example, mother is feminine, and father is masculine. Other times the gender can be arbitrary. A tree is feminine, a branch is masculine, and a leaf is neuter. In these cases, you might have to look up the word to know its gender.

Number

is Singular (S) or Plural (Pl). Either it is one of something or more than one.

Case

determines how a word is functioning in a clause. English nouns do not have cases, but pronouns often have a subject form, possessive form, and an object form:

Nominative

is the subject form. This is used for the subject itself and any renaming of the subject.

Genitive

is often the possessive form, but more generally it makes a noun function like an adjective, modifying another noun. It is often translated with an "of" before the noun.

Dative

is the indirect object case. It is often translated with a "to" or "for" before the noun.

Accusative

is the main object case. This is used for direct objects as well as the objects of some prepositions.

Ablative

is the adverbial case. It is used to make a noun function like an adverb. The noun can be by itself or the object of a preposition. In both cases it will typically be translated as a prepositional phrase.

Vocative

is the direct address case. It used to call address someone in direct speech.

Noun Declensions

are the patterns that nouns and adjectives follow. If English had noun declensions you could say the first declension makes nouns plural by adding an "-s" at the end of the word (dog/dogs). The second declension would make nouns plural by changing the vowels (goose/geese), the third declension would have the same form for singular and plural (deer/deer), the fourth declension would make a word plural by adding "-en" to then end (ox/oxen). If I told you tooth, foot, and man were nouns of the second declension, you could infer the plurals to be teeth, feet, and men.

Noun Declensions

A noun’s declension is determined by the genitive form of the word.

  • The first declension has a genitive singular form of -ae.

  • The second declension has a genitive singular form of -ī.

  • The third declension has a genitive singular form of -is.

  • The fourth declension has a genitive singular form of -ūs.

  • The fifth declension has a genitive singular form of -eī.

Adjectives

modify nouns and must agree with the noun they are modifying.

  • Adjectives can be 1st/2nd declension or 3rd declension.

    • 1st/2nd declension adjectives follow the 1st declension for feminine forms and the 2nd declension for masculine and neuter forms.

    • 3rd declension adjectives have the same forms for masculine and feminine, but neuter forms are different.

  • Adjectives will be glossed by the masculine, feminine, and neuter forms. These can be seen on page 324 of Ecce Romani II

Examples of Adjectives From Ecce Romani I

magnus, -a, -um, big, great, large, loud (voice, laugh) (4, 34)

This is short for magnus, magna, magnum. The -us, -a, -um endings indicate it is a 1st/2nd declension adjective, and it will follow that pattern.

omnis, -is, -e, all, the whole, every, each (6, 18)

This short for omnis, omnis, omne. The -is, -is, -e endings indicate it is a 3rd declension adjective, and it will follow that pattern.

Verbs

express an action or a state of being. Some broad categories for verbs include:

active, ditransitive verbs

active verbs with a direct object and indirect object

active, transitive verbs

active verbs with a direct object

active, intransitive verbs

active verbs without a direct object

stative verbs

intransitive verbs that denote a state of being rather than an action

copular verbs

intransitive verbs that link the subject to the subject complement

In Latin verbs take person, number, tense, voice, and mood.

Person refers to the subject of the verb.
1st person (1st)

the subject is the speaker (I or we)

2nd person (2nd)

the subject is the person being spoken to (you)

3rd person (3rd)

the subject is someone other than the speaker or the person being spoken to (he, she, it, they)

Number refers to the number of subjects.
Singular (S)

there is one subject (I, you, he, she, it)

Plural (Pl)

the subject is the person being spoken to (we, you, they)

Tense in Latin refers to the time and aspect of the action.
time

can be past, present, or future.

aspect

can be continuous or completed (perfected)

List of Verb Tenses

Continuous Aspect
Present Tense (Pres)

is the present continual tense. I walk. I am walking. I do walk.

Imperfect Tense (Imperf)

is the past continual tense. I was walking, I walked.

Future Tense (Fut)

is the future continual tense. I will walk.

Completed Aspect
Perfect Tense (Perf)

the present completed tense. I have walked, I walked.

Pluperfect Tense (Plup)

the past completed tense. I had walked.

Future Perfect Tense (Fut Perf)

the future completed tense. I will have walked.

Voice refers to if the subject is doing the action. (This was not covered in Latin 1, but is covered in the first semester of Latin 2)

Active Voice (Act)

means the subject is the one doing the action.

Passive Voice (Pass)

means the subject is receiving the action.

Mood refers to if the attitude of the verb.

Indicative Mood (Ind)

is the most standard mood. It is just making at statement. Most of the verbs you have seen in Latin have been indicative. The majority of the forms on page 331 are indicative. All of the examples above for tense are indicative.

Imperative Mood (Imp)

is used for commands. In English, these often have an understood subject. There are two types of imperatives on page 331. Walk.

Infinitive Mood (Inf)

is a verb used to complete the main verb or it is used as a noun. Not everyone considers infinitives a mood, but it is easier to classify them as such. Infinitives are verbs that function as nouns. The present, active infinitive is the 2nd principal part and is the first line on page 331.

Subjunctive Mood (Subj)

is used in many ways. It can be thought of as being one step removed from reality. It is something that should be or could be done. We might get to the subjunctive by the end of the school year.
